Overview

Phishing emails attempt to trick you into revealing passwords, downloading malware, or sharing sensitive information. In healthcare, successful phishing attacks can expose Protected Health Information (PHI) and trigger HIPAA breach notifications affecting thousands of patients. Your vigilance is our strongest defense.

How to Identify a Phishing Email

Look for these red flags:

üö©
Suspicious sender address

The display name may look legitimate, but the actual email address is wrong. Example: "IT Support" but the address is it-support@g00gle-corp.com

üö©
Urgency and pressure tactics

"Your account will be locked in 24 hours!" or "Immediate action required!" ‚Äî real IT communications don't threaten you.

üö©
Suspicious links

Hover (don't click!) over links. If the URL doesn't match the claimed destination, it's likely phishing. Watch for misspelled domains.

üö©
Unexpected attachments

Especially .exe, .zip, .js, or Office files asking to "Enable Macros." Never open attachments you didn't expect.

üö©
Requests for credentials or personal info

Legitimate IT staff and services will never ask for your password via email.

üö©
Grammar and formatting issues

Professional organizations don't send emails with poor grammar, odd formatting, or mismatched branding.

How to Report a Suspicious Email

Method 1: Use the Phish Alert Button (Preferred)

1

Click on the email to select it (do NOT click any links inside it).

2

3

Click "Report" in the confirmation dialog. The email will be forwarded to IT Security and removed from your inbox.

1

2

If You Already Clicked a Link or Opened an Attachment

üö® Act Immediately ‚Äî Time is Critical
1
Disconnect from the network

Turn off Wi-Fi or unplug your ethernet cable to prevent potential malware from spreading.

2
Call IT Security immediately

Call ext. 4357 and say "security incident." Do not email ‚Äî use the phone.

3
Change your password from a different device

If you entered credentials on a phishing page, change your password immediately from your phone or another computer.

4
Do NOT delete the email

IT Security needs the email for forensic analysis. Leave it in your inbox.

5
Document what happened

Write down what you clicked, what information you entered (if any), and the approximate time. This helps IT assess the scope.

Healthcare-Specific Phishing Scenarios

Be especially alert for these healthcare-targeted phishing types:

  • Fake EHR login pages: "Your IntakeQ session has expired ‚Äî click here to log in again"
  • Insurance/billing scams: "Updated billing codes attached ‚Äî review required by EOD"
  • Compliance threats: "HIPAA audit finding ‚Äî immediate response required"
  • Vendor impersonation: Fake emails from EHR vendors, medical supply companies, or insurance providers
  • Patient data requests: "Please send patient records for [name]" from an external address
